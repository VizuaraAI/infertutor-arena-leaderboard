Anatomy of the score

The metric isn't arbitrary — every term maps to something a real serving system is judged on. Read it and the whole leaderboard makes sense.

Goodputthroughput × (1 − error)

Chunks per second that actually reached users. A fast server that drops requests earns nothing for them — errors are not goodput.

Usersconcurrent load

How many simultaneous users were served. More users = more reward, so it pays to scale — but only if latency holds.

TTFT p95time to first token

The 95th-percentile wait before the first token appears. In the denominator: responsiveness is rewarded, lag is punished.

ITL p95inter-token latency

The 95th-percentile gap between streamed tokens — how smooth the text feels. The single biggest lever in the whole arena.

GPUshardware spent

Total GPUs deployed. Dividing by it means efficiency beats brute force — four tuned GPUs can outscore eight.

How we verified

Same formula for everyone — and we ran it ourselves where we could.

For the eight engineers who submitted their benchmark result JSONs, we computed the score directly with the workshop's official score_infertutor.py — no trust required. For the rest, we recomputed the same formula from the best-config metrics in their report and labelled it self-reported. What the winners did — and why

The leaderboard is the scoreboard; this is the playbook. Seven patterns separated the top runs — each a direct consequence of what the score rewards.

01

Compiled CUDA graphs are the master lever

Turning on compiled mode (CUDA-graph capture, --no-fast-boot) collapsed inter-token latency from ~20 ms to ~5–6 ms. Because ITL is in the denominator, that one switch was roughly a 24× score jump — the biggest single move anyone made.

Seen in — Manoj · Jahnavi · Sharan · Hemanth · Ankit · Oluwaseyi
02

Fewer, well-tuned GPUs beat brute force

The score divides by GPU count, so efficiency per GPU wins. Two-to-four tuned GPUs beat eight; a single warmed H100 beat two replicas; one compiled GPU beat an 8-GPU eager cluster.

Seen in — Sharan · Bhabani · Do Giang · Prashant
03

Your benchmark can lie — measure from inside

A slow off-cluster / home-uplink client bufferbloated TTFT ~3× and faked a 22 ms ITL. Co-locating the client inside the datacenter tripled the score on the identical server. The measurement, not the model, was the bug.

Seen in — Hemanth · Sharan
04

Kill prefix caching for a tiny system prompt

At a 40-token prefix, cache bookkeeping cost more than it saved — about a 28% TTFT penalty left on. Caching isn't free; match it to prompt length.

Seen in — Manoj · Jahnavi · Sharan
05

Find the latency knee (~100 users/GPU)

Past the knee, queuing makes TTFT explode (a Little's-Law demo went 1.3 s → 11.4 s). One engineer's roofline model predicted the bottleneck order before any run. Push load to the elbow, not past it.

Seen in — Ankit · Prashant · Ishani
06

Warm up, ramp up, run long enough

Cold-start contaminates p95: a warmup fix cut TTFT 6.2 s → 1.85 s; a 120 s ramp let replicas finish graph compilation before peak; 90 s tests misled where 180 s told the truth.

Seen in — Rahul · Oluwaseyi · Raghavan
07

Think in rooflines — and stay honest

The strongest reports placed every config in the memory-bound roofline and disqualified their own inflated runs. Knowing the hardware ceiling you chase, and trusting only clean measurements, is itself the engineering.

Seen in — Ishani · Bhabani · Aman
